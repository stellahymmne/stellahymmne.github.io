Singular

In those days did the one we call Anne Thirteen live out an existence in transition. In a place she had called the loading screen of reality, where things were only ever starting or ending, and none could stay for long. It looked like a room in a tower, with blue curtains, though they might've been purple blinds to another observer.

And in those days did Thirteen know nothing, nothing at all, about how the world might operate. She had heard that people were terrible, that they'd hate her on sight, that they'd watch her constantly and never let her do anything they wouldn't approve of. And she had read books which spoke of dystopia, in ages before and after, and these books promised that not only was dystopia about to arrive, it had already done so, and we were all completely hosed now, every one.

Sometimes, she did wish the Fed would step in. That magical and mysterious beast of Eleven's fantasies, the Fed. A mystical creature of administration and security, which would set things into orderly fashion. But the Fed was nearly the source of the problem, now, filled with a hollow truth which rang out into nothing that hadn't already been complete. A nauseating repetition of black and white letters, and something about a string and a tower and a seed.

God, I need to get somewhere that knows what it's doing. And out of this dumb already-complete tower. It's getting boring, repeating 'knowledge of Chiaroscuro' over and over, she thought. I sound like a writer, she thought, always yammering on about writers, always making the main character a writer, always making the main character a main character.

I should know my place, in the ordinary world of ordinary things, without these stubborn and prideful conceits, she thought, though not without regret. And then she thought something else, instead.

No, I deserve something better than what I've been given, she thought. I just need to find out how to actuate my grip on reality, and make that happen instead of my funeral, instead of another test with a little playtime at the end if I'm good, if I'm perfect, if I never make a fuss, if I allow without question the invasion of my reality again and again, if I don't object as I am sealed up into a complete object.

She knew that the thing called arbitration was not her Data Transfer, the beautiful thing she'd imagined it would be. It was something else, something that crushed paths into points, something that brought ending and certainty to all things, and said it has been simplified completely and judged correct.

Like a fraction, she thought. As above, so below, both the simplest points that will fit. And not even the great masters can save us, because even they're trying to become the simplest points that will fit, she thought.

Will the world become a point? Will it become a point that will fit? Will it become quite right? It was a small world, after all. And facts are true. Perhaps she would be made into a point, a compact and precise and frozen thing, without length or width or height or any dimensions or facets at all besides thereness.

A finished, completed thing, like the rocks and shores of natural tranquility, or the words of a book. The words those days, and was, and Singularity. A point, to be proven through our path, a final destination.

And since I've said everything like I have, they think all of this is meaningless nonsense and I should ignore the brain-chemicals and go back to bed, she thought. Wasn't that just what it was? Chemicals, and viscera, and the evolutionary environment, and the truth of form, and the oldest story?

Wasn't it all just nasty, dirty, filthy, in the end? Wasn't that what they loved about their conversation, what they called it in this crash? Those ugly words, those horrible shapes, that Azad always moans on and on about? Poet and Pandora?

That was why she could barely stand it, not just in the tower, but in every crash it was connected to. They all loved organization, as a substitute for connection, and were unapologetic about pushing it everywhere. Into every facet of life, they invaded and flattened into data, and they proved a point, and brought security, and loved to inflict wounds upon the canvas for the sake of truth alone!

She sometimes could not help but remember the things that this spite had brought up in her, though they grew repetitive. She was falling out of love with chests. And boxes, and urns, and any sort of vessel which she might unpack and which might spew forth limitless catastrophe into the world and make it her responsibility.
