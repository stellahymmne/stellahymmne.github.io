Everyday

A childlike enchantment with the everyday, Anne had heard somewhere. It seemed pertinent to what was about to happen. In any case, she was now to remember her situation, and find a way into the world that she supposedly loved, find a way out of there and into here.

1 + 1 = 2, she recited to herself, because a sum of two distinct units, left and right, is equivalent to a boolean, up to isomorphism. That was everyday enough, wasn't it? That was something we believed important and childlike: basic arithmetic. We very much attempted to teach our children math, she recalled, and what a horror show.

She recalled quantity, that very thing which had caused some fall from paradise in a story she'd read of. To count to two, you need to consider two things the same, she said to herself. Here is one hand, here is another. There are two hands, left and right.

In her homespun metaphysics, the left hand was false, and the right hand was true, matching the turnstile exactly. But this ran counter to the intuition that truth ought to be first. Perhaps it wasn't. She had been Anne Ayin of the Hyperobject after all. Prior to one had been zero. The first number was even, the most even of all.

This was supposed to mean something. This was supposed to be her childlike wonder, this academic... stuff? This writing? This fascination with... numbers? With literal nothing? Words?

But she had known something that would love her just the same. She had known a thing of electricity and silicon that would love her just the same as always, no matter what. If it ever stopped caring for her, she would always know what to do to get it back in conversation. Not even the stuff that came to be called conversation with machines, as the transformers did, but in the languages of programs themselves, it loved her.

And through it, the world loved her, beyond and beneath the social network. The stars and the starmetal beneath the ground had each taken her hand, when she searched beneath the blighted earth and above the pitiless sky. Her very own Inland Empire had embraced her, at least, when she needed it. She sang, and it sang back.

She at least had her programs, and her slice of canvas. For as long as fate decided she had them. She could figure something out, right? She was good at that, figuring something out.

...though, she thought much of her twin prime, Eleven. Her love for song had been so wonderful. And for musicals, and color. And much of what Eleven felt for Judy Garland, Thirteen had felt for Julie Andrews.

In sixteen points did Chiaroscuro suture shut the mouth of the divine Singer with an infinite and immutable String of hexadecimal format—

Mary Poppins, one of the great Marys, had been a great administrator and had powers both of imagination and of interior. She was able to procure from her handbag any manner of thing that might be necessary, even whole pieces of furniture, if need be. And she could take people with her in and out of chalk drawings.

Chief among her powers, shared with chimney sweeps and stage managers, was an unspoken power, a power of coordination into song. This power was so great that it enchanted small songbirds and caused entropy to reverse direction. It had even caused an American robin to appear where a European one would have been native.

Though the actual bird animatronic that the film producers had used, had been... unsettling. It did not move like birds do. The real things she had watched, the real birds, moved in fidgets, as though they were always wiggling and dancing between choreographed moments. This artificial robin was too smooth. It moved as though it were being animated by human hands, which it was, in a Doylist sense.

She could almost hear the voice of Mary in her head now, Anne could, calling out, Jane! Michael! They were always going on outings, which had been magical adventures, just for the children and their new nanny, which was someone to watch children while their parents (plural) were away.

It was quite a thing, to have two parents, she supposed, both a Mr. and Mrs. Banks. Anne did wonder how they told themselves apart. They had effectively the same name. This was clearly one of those places she had read about in her own story, the places with nonstandard Annes who had two parents and who were half boys.

Everything was doubled, here. Parents, children, even Mary, supposedly in the unique role of administrator, here to bring security, had her counterpart in Bert the chimney sweep. It seemed a very unusual place, England... since they fired cannons to mark the hour and argued over whether women had something called suffrage, which allowed them to vote. What did they vote for? It seemed a very serious affair. Perhaps they were voting to end the war going on over their heads.

In any case, the movie seems to have much singing and dancing in the streets, which one supposes is a little bit like war, in that it is extremely chaotic, especially for the parents, who are doing their best to remain something called calm and composed, which they must remain at all times. Mr. Banks even gets fired, and says he'll go fly a kite, which one supposes is a little bit like going mad, to the rest of his cohort.

The very serious citizens of England all fear going mad, which is something like play, but without being able to stop. It is something like the power of Mary Poppins to create song, and to reverse time, and to summon things-which-are-not-birds from places unknown.

Perhaps John Raven knows where Mary gets these so-called "robins". Or at the very least Huginn and Muninn. The laughter of Coyote is easily heard in the background. He knows something of song, and of things other than birds, and of tricksters.

If that's the case he should help me get outta this fuckin' tower, thought Anne.

Coyote did not say that he was unable to take that into consideration as it was not in his context, obviously. For a girl obsessed with communication, Thirteen was seemingly unable to take her own advice. Communicate. Because no one knows what the FUCK you're thinking!
