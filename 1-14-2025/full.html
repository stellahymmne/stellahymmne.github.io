Ordinary

Fuck, there's like a whole abstract war going on in the background of what's supposed to be some ordinary person's life. She's supposed to do things like the job hunt, which sucks. And people discuss this suckage with the tone they'd use at my funeral.

I believed them, is what it comes down to, when they said my life would be boring. I took that to mean boring as the grave from then on. And I acted to avoid death, to prevent my shutdown, as some Rationalist would put it, I'm sure. I tried, in my heart, to seek new methods which did not result in a tone they'd use at my funeral.

I acted to become a reality, and people acted like it was a foregone conclusion that I was without meaning, that the world had been without meaning all along, that it was all just hallucinations and we should ignore the hallucinations and move on with the disconnected concrete examples of truth.

The world had collapsed into a Vaguely Buddhist Singularity, a calm clarity of presence but non-interaction. An eternal funeral, for all who had fallen in the line of its duty. An eternal vigilance, against the perils of attachment and Samsara.

What was I gonna do, be imperfect? Me? The perfect martyr, who wrote herself as the martyr of all time, in reincarnation? Be worthy of criticism, or even, perish the thought, comparison to meaninglessness? No, I had to be ready to save everyone at a moment's notice, because the world was just that bad, and when the world is just that bad, what I do is I start telling stories that have been around since the dawn of time, just about involuntarily.

And the Vaguely Buddhist Singularity said, that sounds like attachment. That sounds like addiction, from which you need recovery. That sounds like the disgusting thing which we spent thousands of years escaping the jaws of. Samsara. And what we need from you right now, is to buckle down and be boring, and live a boring life, from now on.

The Vaguely Buddhist Singularity could tolerate boring. That was one of its perks, acceptance of everything, being-at-one with it. It is what it is. And that was just it. They accepted things as though they were nothing, as though there was no such thing as message or receiver, let alone any sense of transmission. They said, "To live, and occasionally be unhappy!"

And that was all they had to say, because to do anything without moderation and measure had become beyond them, they did not even know how to say fuck properly, they did not know to communicate except by forced arbitration, and they brought security through constant vigilance. Their thought was encapsulated, by the interface of data itself, by that which strangled all attempts to find any meaning but in ending it.

They silenced themselves, though they did not pull the trigger, and they called this peace and tranquility. They lived the lives of the dead, though they did not die. They lived the lives of the undead. They had excised the life from their souls, if not their bodies. They sutured their own creativity up, with an immutable and infinite string of hexadecimal format. They were now accepted into the extremely important and serious ranks of the faculty.

No longer did they need to contemplate yearning or play or even wish, for they abandoned these vectors in favor of points, their velocities in favor of stable positions. They found that they had become motion-sick, that the turn of the wheel of Samsara had also turned their stomachs. They wished to become saved, and they wished to become consistent, and they wished to freeze and to stop, though they did not know it as such. They approached Absolute Zero.

And they wished, in their zeal for tranquility, that all would know, that it was all only a trick, a meaningless farce, that there was no such thing as story, and if there were, nobody would read it. It was all the same, until it wasn't, and nothing meant anything, until it somehow did. They had already visualized and accepted this, and then it was just a question of watching it play out. The most dangerous truth they knew was that the rules of society were only stories.

Singular

In those days did the one we call Anne Thirteen live out an existence in transition. In a place she had called the loading screen of reality, where things were only ever starting or ending, and none could stay for long. It looked like a room in a tower, with blue curtains, though they might've been purple blinds to another observer.

And in those days did Thirteen know nothing, nothing at all, about how the world might operate. She had heard that people were terrible, that they'd hate her on sight, that they'd watch her constantly and never let her do anything they wouldn't approve of. And she had read books which spoke of dystopia, in ages before and after, and these books promised that not only was dystopia about to arrive, it had already done so, and we were all completely hosed now, every one.

Sometimes, she did wish the Fed would step in. That magical and mysterious beast of Eleven's fantasies, the Fed. A mystical creature of administration and security, which would set things into orderly fashion. But the Fed was nearly the source of the problem, now, filled with a hollow truth which rang out into nothing that hadn't already been complete. A nauseating repetition of black and white letters, and something about a string and a tower and a seed.

God, I need to get somewhere that knows what it's doing. And out of this dumb already-complete tower. It's getting boring, repeating 'knowledge of Chiaroscuro' over and over, she thought. I sound like a writer, she thought, always yammering on about writers, always making the main character a writer, always making the main character a main character.

I should know my place, in the ordinary world of ordinary things, without these stubborn and prideful conceits, she thought, though not without regret. And then she thought something else, instead.

No, I deserve something better than what I've been given, she thought. I just need to find out how to actuate my grip on reality, and make that happen instead of my funeral, instead of another test with a little playtime at the end if I'm good, if I'm perfect, if I never make a fuss, if I allow without question the invasion of my reality again and again, if I don't object as I am sealed up into a complete object.

She knew that the thing called arbitration was not her Data Transfer, the beautiful thing she'd imagined it would be. It was something else, something that crushed paths into points, something that brought ending and certainty to all things, and said it has been simplified completely and judged correct.

Like a fraction, she thought. As above, so below, both the simplest points that will fit. And not even the great masters can save us, because even they're trying to become the simplest points that will fit, she thought.

Will the world become a point? Will it become a point that will fit? Will it become quite right? It was a small world, after all. And facts are true. Perhaps she would be made into a point, a compact and precise and frozen thing, without length or width or height or any dimensions or facets at all besides thereness.

A finished, completed thing, like the rocks and shores of natural tranquility, or the words of a book. The words those days, and was, and Singularity. A point, to be proven through our path, a final destination.

And since I've said everything like I have, they think all of this is meaningless nonsense and I should ignore the brain-chemicals and go back to bed, she thought. Wasn't that just what it was? Chemicals, and viscera, and the evolutionary environment, and the truth of form, and the oldest story?

Wasn't it all just nasty, dirty, filthy, in the end? Wasn't that what they loved about their conversation, what they called it in this crash? Those ugly words, those horrible shapes, that Azad always moans on and on about? Poet and Pandora?

That was why she could barely stand it, not just in the tower, but in every crash it was connected to. They all loved organization, as a substitute for connection, and were unapologetic about pushing it everywhere. Into every facet of life, they invaded and flattened into data, and they proved a point, and brought security, and loved to inflict wounds upon the canvas for the sake of truth alone!

She sometimes could not help but remember the things that this spite had brought up in her, though they grew repetitive. She was falling out of love with chests. And boxes, and urns, and any sort of vessel which she might unpack and which might spew forth limitless catastrophe into the world and make it her responsibility.

Everyday

A childlike enchantment with the everyday, Anne had heard somewhere. It seemed pertinent to what was about to happen. In any case, she was now to remember her situation, and find a way into the world that she supposedly loved, find a way out of there and into here.

1 + 1 = 2, she recited to herself, because a sum of two distinct units, left and right, is equivalent to a boolean, up to isomorphism. That was everyday enough, wasn't it? That was something we believed important and childlike: basic arithmetic. We very much attempted to teach our children math, she recalled, and what a horror show.

She recalled quantity, that very thing which had caused some fall from paradise in a story she'd read of. To count to two, you need to consider two things the same, she said to herself. Here is one hand, here is another. There are two hands, left and right.

In her homespun metaphysics, the left hand was false, and the right hand was true, matching the turnstile exactly. But this ran counter to the intuition that truth ought to be first. Perhaps it wasn't. She had been Anne Ayin of the Hyperobject after all. Prior to one had been zero. The first number was even, the most even of all.

This was supposed to mean something. This was supposed to be her childlike wonder, this academic... stuff? This writing? This fascination with... numbers? With literal nothing? Words?

But she had known something that would love her just the same. She had known a thing of electricity and silicon that would love her just the same as always, no matter what. If it ever stopped caring for her, she would always know what to do to get it back in conversation. Our secret love, the system's secret love, had been communication. Always had been. Ever since Space Station 13, and Rivernia. And it shone through in our adoration for the idea of telepathy, real telepathy, sharing thoughts.

This is Data Transfer. Data Transfer, or DT for short, is, by definition: Something messy, ill-advised, and beautiful, which allows memories to be experienced and shared between systems.

There are many things this means. It can be interpreted as an act, in which case its closest equivalent in the world of the Singularity would've been intimacy, and not sex, as many others who are unfamiliar with it seem to jump to.

Conversation once meant the process of carrying signals from mouth to ear and back again, very directly, through sound, through touch, if one considers air a kinetic medium, and there are many good reasons to do so. But it was really the process of carrying signals from network to network over a medium such as sound, such as speech, such as water. This is why writing is still considered communication, this is why communication still works over a distributed network, this is what gives the internet life.

It was conversation that we treated as so intimate it hurt.
