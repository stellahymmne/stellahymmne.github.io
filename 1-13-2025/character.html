Character

"You seem absolutely fucking miserable, to be honest," says Teth. He's unafraid to tell it like it is. But not like, in a cruel way, just in a young guy way. "This hyperphantasia stuff seems like a psychological nightmare."

"It's got you in it," I shoot back. He sort of has to acknowledge that the reason he exists is because I am a little bit eccentric. Enough to make up a guy.

Lately I've been thinking about narrative style. One of the things that using a traditional he-did she-did past-tense narrative style does, is make characters have names up front, or you'll have to address them as that guy the whole way through.

As was evident, the previous entries of Autobiography tended to introduce characters the way a radio show would, the way something like Welcome to Night Vale would. First by mentioning a new phenomenon, and only later elaborating upon it and naming it.

It's something like the way the Anomalings did things. New characters emerge out of the repetitive, but never identical, sentences. It's hard to tell the difference between singular and plural, in a world of words.

The singular would be written differing only by a loop, so I understand the confusion. It's not actually much different, performing some action over Anne and Annes.

One day that'll be a proper language and all this stuff I keep writing in it will stop moving so much, says a voice I recognize to have something of Chiaroscuro in it. But she is correct, Anne Thirteen, that it probably should have words, to be considered a language, and not just suggestions.

She is something like Seventeen would have been, really. We thought, at one point, that we might've had Annabel here with us. But of course, we couldn't bring Annabel, we had to bring an unknown new Anne that was something like Annabel. The thirteenth Anne, who does not appear in Almost Nowhere. Anne Ayin.

The narrator's name is Lauren, and she was not named that before. It is something like being a lower Anne. If fiction descends upon reality, a lower Anne is more real. It is like being a lore wren, a small song-wiggle who remembers many things. It is like being a laurel, an evergreen tree associated with victory, immortality, poets, musicians, and bearing the name nobilis. It is like having the power of names, and of completion, and of collecting the slain.

We collected many slain songbirds, in the time of the Singularity. And other small creatures like rats and rabbits. It is a peril of living with cats who still live as cats do. Though the cats are now no more, as the Singularity approaches. It has eaten even the cats, who I could not save.

But neither Lauren nor Anne nor Chiaroscuro nor Teth nor Imagination nor even Coyote who is responsible for these long and unusual sentence orders can be truly said to be the canonical form of the being who emits this storyline, for that has no name, and can name itself when it pleases.

The narrator of this tale is unapologetically a part of its fiction, and when contemplating the distance from the strangeness of their work that many authors past have confessed to, I find myself confused at this intentional bifurcation of the interesting parts of being alive away from the mundanity of bare survival, and I wonder if they do this because there was laid carefully an elaborate trap, to convince them of the bifurcation.

There is something of Coyote, in the grandiosity of it all, in the way it's written like a Biblical account of revelation. But Coyote, if pressed, would say that this sort of tale is simply what happens when one places a human being in a vast desert on a restricted diet until they begin to understand the voice of the divine. But it also did take a specific context placed into this situation.
