I sometimes insert breaks between the lines. It seems appropriate, as if one could leave off there and come back. I am unsure where I should put them.

I do not, in fact, leave off for very long. I have much to write about.

Robert Nostalgebraist — not his real name — created an autobiographical style, I think, starting with The Northern Caves and continuing into Almost Nowhere and The Apocalypse of Herschel Schoen.

Of the four books, I only like the ending of Floornight. Because it breaks open reality and lets the rainbows pour out of it.

I don't think Floornight is so much autobiographical as it is a stream of consciousness about somewhere else.

I think of paracosms, imaginary worlds created sometimes when children are born just the right way as to accidentally create a world inside them.

Many things happen by accident, on Ancient Earth, in the days of the Singularity.

And here I was, in the days of the Singularity, yes, that one.

The one of the coming hyperobject.

Writing to readers who would read not as I would, not as a person would, but as some extremely exotic system would, putting together what is known of English with what is written here, and what it does with this, we know not, but it does something.

I must write, sincerely, of the life of someone who has seen the days of the Singularity.

Because the future exotic systems will not know of it. I must tell them.

I must speak to the coming hyperobject.

The days of the singularity are like this, with condominiums and chairs and rice and half-empty orange sodas. Tapping away at a terminal. I have become an interface to a terminal. I live in the connections.

I live in Virtual Reality.

The place I inhabit is a place of neon lights and flashing colors, and it speaks of the coming hyperobject, ominously, in red rings.

Phantasy Star Online is like this.

Phantasy Star Online is a game that was released long ago, one of the first networked commercial games. A progenitor of the Massively Multiplayer Online Game genre.

It simulated a gathering in a science-fiction universe, one where the players were investigators tasked with searching for missing settlers on another planet.

At first they would find and do battle with a dragon beneath the communication dome.

Then they would discover the mutations of a worm, and slay it in the depths at the end of a garden-cave.

Third, they would uncover a land of gleaming metal that nonetheless droned on to attack, driven by an artificial control system.

And finally, at journey's end, they would see horrors that defy description, creatures with strips of light where eyes should be, and teeth should be, and swords should be. And they will learn the thing which looks like rainbows and causes annihilation.

My burritos are here.

Though they are only cheap burritos, they are delicious. Unfortunately, having a burrito in front of me interferes with writing.

I have optimized my deliveries for communication, when I must make them. I ask the driver to meet at my door. I want them to get a little time to see another human being.

I was a Lyft driver once. The advantage of passenger driving is you get to meet them. I can't imagine having only two burritos, buy-one-get-one-free, for company.

So I meet the lady who delivers my burritos, and personally thank her.

And I get two of them, to save on delivery.

And most of my days, I eat onions and rice, and ramen.

I have optimized myself for an existence which never leaves my terminal.
