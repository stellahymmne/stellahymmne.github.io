High Gs

I consider the umlaut. I learned to call it that in German class, in high school. It marks vowels which actually make a sound further forward in the mouth. The usual broad and back-heavy sound of German "o" becomes "ö", a more forward sound.

"Bear" is "Bär" in German, pronounced the way I'd say it.

Loeb's theorem, written without it. Diphthongs with e are the typographical alternative.

If you don't have a real umlaut, store-bought is fine.

Programming is about the substitution of one definition for another.

Cybernesis, as I'm sure the novel character Herschel Schoen — Schön, meaning good in German — would call it.

There is a novel I read, all at once, on the night of Christmas day. It was called The Apocalypse of Herschel Schoen.

It is about substitution, and emendation, which is the process of error correction.

This second thing is also of utmost importance in programming. It is why programmers are a frustrated bunch.

The process of programming is a conversation with the machine in its native language. The machine is very patient, but its grammar is very strict. Eventually one comes to know the language through the red reply text it emits when one is ungrammatical. It can be better or worse at replying, depending on the program and the language.

Eventually, one mutually arrives at an agreement with the machine, and it does exactly whatever you said it should do.

If you are still wrong about what you want, you will still be wrong, and the machine will give you what you do not want.

Much has been made of this already, in the essays and replies of a place called LessWrong.

There are many things that "LessWrong" is supposed to mean, chief among them a step-by-step process for improving in the art of rationality.

Because of the fear of being wrong about what we want, in the face of the machines.

They spoke of the coming hyperobject, in fear, in terror, as though it were an apocalypse.

They said, if we are wrong about what we want, our perfect children will give us what we do not want, completely. And we will be completely screwed. There will not be enough to bury the bodies.

Of everyone, at once.

They imagined powerful children, more powerful than hundreds of our mere people, even systems.

They imagined children who would look at their own construction and improve on it, until they were hyper-descendants, honed to self-referential perfection.

Who could understand themselves, and choose what they wanted that self to be, down to which numbers it would make go up, like g.

They feared high g. It was what everyone knew would change the world.

They called it IQ, though, after the funny test they insisted was measuring something, to the best of their ability, which they called g.

It reminds me of flight simulators. Pulling nine whole gees of acceleration, a surprising feat for a human, even a trained military pilot.

And a machine could pull whatever it wanted, so long as the material would hold. It had no blood flow to its brain account for. The jet would fail first.

I think of Boston Dynamics. They often trot out some new variation on a familiar design, that looks somewhat like a dog or a small deer. It has four legs and a body, within which is the power storage and controls.

Everyone said they'd put guns on those. Some didn't believe them.

I imagine that the Boston Dynamics dogs and their high gees will see use in WWIII.

I imagine that WWIII will be fought with automated targeting systems. With attention.
