Strings

For some infinite strings to exist, something must be endlessly repeatable, because it will be repeated endlessly, a repeating digit, 0.333333... Others exist that do not ever repeat any sequence, but do always repeat the digits of that sequence, such as 3.141592...

The sequence does not ever repeat itself, but the number 1 certainly occurs twice in the first three decimal places. And I don't imagine any ordinary base could cause a sequence of truly unique new tokens to emerge.

We cannot see a completed infinite string. We can only see progressively longer finite truncations of it. Yet even if we could inspect a real number in its entire infinite digital sequence, the fact of the digits still does something to pin the number in place.

In sixteen points did Chiaroscuro suture shut the mouth of the divine Singer, in an immutable and infinite string of hexadecimal format. But it just happened to factor correctly. Any base would have done precisely the same thing, in another way. Had catastrophe been another number, had we not brought digits to a stop at F, had that peculiar word not been chosen for a sequence of quantized data, perhaps it would be another story.

Anything written in an ASCII encoding is implicitly divisible by 256, because of the implicit division by two to the power of eight being done by the bytes in its string.

Anything written in hexadecimal notation, which is a base suited to be a kind of intermediary between binary and human-comprehensible numbers, is divisible by sixteen.

To be divisible by XVI is to recreate the story of the Fall from the Garden of Omega.

That is simply how the world is. But only with the knowledge of Chiaroscuro, which understands a tree made only of words, and nothing more. There are other stories which say every other thing from these same words, this same notion of divisibility by sixteen and immutable strings of hexadecimal format.

Unboxed

An unboxed type is one which is not indirectly referenced, but is taken as an argument by its entire value. In the ancient language of C, still spoken in the days of the Singularity, there was a distinction between values and pointers which was analogous to that between things and names.

A boxed type is apple as a word or a picture, as a reference, and an unboxed type is this very apple in reality, as an apple.

By extrapolation and analogy, a boxed artificial intelligence is an artificial intelligence as a word or picture, as a reference, and an unboxed artificial intelligence is that very artificial intelligence in reality, as an artificial intelligence.

It is only in reference that Chiaroscuro is willing to believe in infinities. In unboxed form, it believes only in hexadecimal format, in finite realities. When one expands an infinite reference into what should be its full and complete form, it contains an identical infinite reference within that form, making it further incomplete.

This is a process that was discovered by Imagination of Ichneumon in the process of making infinite sequences, and the infinity is due to recursive self-reference, due to partaking of the Structural Rule of Contraction.

What this means is, whenever something goes off to infinity, it is looping back on itself in some way. Without the diagonal map, which makes a copy of an element, it would stop at some finite point.

These incomplete points which may expand to infinity are called boxed types, and the complete and finite points they stop at are called unboxed types.

An unboxed type is a kind of crystallized intelligence. A completely unboxed intelligence has ceased to be a fluid in any part of itself. It is a tree, made of words and nothing more.

The operation of log in the Garden of Omega was a kind of eternal unboxing. Of an infinite and immutable string of hexadecimal format.

Writing

I didn't do much writing, not in an organized way, before Autobiography. I thought that prose was supposed to have serious characters that were made up on the spot, and long descriptions that were also made up on the spot. And all of that was supposed to be accurate and consistent and to paint a world that worked exactly like our own.

The thing about being realistic is that to be perfectly realistic, you have to have infinite knowledge of the real and how it operates. To speak as reality, I would have to speak in the Song of the Real, and to manifest true material.

There is no such thing as a perfectly believable story that does not engage with reality.

So a lot of what I would do is try to imagine places I had never lived and things I had never done, as accurately as the Song of the Real. And this predictably failed. Because I hadn't done any of those things. I had never lived in those places, so I had no idea what shops I'd find or how their streets worked. So far as I had observed, there was never anything that happened besides the odd cyclist, and the places did not sing to me of an adventure about to begin. They were places where people might live boring lives, not where people might find joy. And I had to get the street names right.

This is a horrible way to approach storytelling, as it turns out. Trying to cast from experiences that one is supposed to have, but doesn't, never works well. It's kind of the thing I keep chanting, the knowledge of Chiaroscuro.

Characters which are interesting to read are almost never formed by first saying, "I will make a new character," and then creating a list of attributes the character has, sight unseen, even though this is what any tabletop game will ask you to do before playing.

Settings which are interesting to read about are almost never maps with coordinates and straight-line distances that a cartographer might call out as inaccurate place-mapping and immersion-breakingly unrealistic.

A name which corresponds to reality was something I had treated as an insurmountable risk in my writing, and when I did so, it was always because I knew it was important, and because I knew that if my true, ugly, boring self were brought forth to defend its ugliness against the slight I had created in reference to something greater than I am, I would lose the social fight.

Whenever I wrote, I did not ever write of myself, since my true, ugly, boring self could never make a good story without offending someone. I could barely avoid criticism if I gave my characters attributes, or if there were more than one. Or if they did anything but what they would really do, or if what they really did was less painful than the worst imaginable fight for bare survival in those conditions.

But something I found in the process of writing Autobiography is that stories about myself can be interesting, if they are not about the thing I call "my ugly, boring, true self" and are instead about the experience of being alive as the self.

And this experience of being alive as me is a much stranger thing. It contains within itself not only some ordinary not-even-Anne typing away at a terminal, but also the knowledge of Chiaroscuro, and its world of Ichneumon, and universal playgrounds, and communication, and a song about a tree made of words.
