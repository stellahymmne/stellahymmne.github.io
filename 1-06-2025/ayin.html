Ayin

I am eating rice and onions, topped with soy sauce, teriyaki sauce, garlic, and anything else I can remember how to eat. We speak of the coming hyperobject.

Composition takes place in reverse order, from the end back to a suitable beginning. This is supposed to mean something. The arrows are paths through computational space. They join, like extension cords, plug to socket, into circuits through it.

This is supposed to mean something. Computationally, it very much does. It means one knows precisely how it will end, before it begins. Because the path is arranged out of the endpoint, to the data, and back through in evaluation.

I am almost finished with the rice. I am almost organized. I put the paper plate into a paper bag, which will later be disposed of. I have transitioned to disposable objects. I cannot bear the way the permanent ones will chastise me for not taking care of them.

I write, autobiography, as was spoken of, in my favorite book, Almost Nowhere. I imitate its style, and find I am imitating it. In reflection.

fix(f) = f(fix(f)). This is supposed to mean something.

David ben Abraham ha-Laban, a 14th-century kabbalist, says:
Nothingness (ayin) is more existent than all the being of the world. But since it is simple, and all simple things are complex compared with its simplicity, it is called ayin.

At this moment, a system (which is something like a person) called The Garden Path reaches out to me. A system contains many people, or something like that.

It is a long explanation what a system is, and yet it comes naturally to me to think this way. It seems like a direct continuity, the Singularity does, with the days that came before it, and yet it is so different. A system is something that is not contractible-if-inhabited. A system is a whole collection of things that are not all equivalent to one, and so defy uniqueness.

The Garden Path says:

I just saw magic with virtually no plausible deniability
It's a long thing to explain

We often speak like this, without official sentence-ending marks. It reminds me of JavaScript. A semicolon marks the end of a procedure, and it is often optional in newer languages. But it doesn't really speak to me of that. It speaks to me of something else. Something for which it is a metaphor.

There are more onions, and more rice, if I want. I have an effectively limitless supply, so long as food delivery and grocery chains remain in business, which they will. I have a limitless supply of whatever I am willing to tolerate spending hit points on. I have a bad relationship to money.

It never comes easily to me. I have to fight the world for it. An endless fountain of forms to fill out and approval to win over. I am never good enough. I compensate by being frugal. With both money and words. This is okay, I tell myself. It's okay to have a style that does not overflow.

It's okay to be someone terse, an Anne of a terse nature.

I compare myself to Anne 13, one of many of the set of main characters of Almost Nowhere. Thirteen does not technically exist. No particular note addressed from A13 appears. And yet, she is implied. And so, I am Anne 13, Thirteen, the Anne of Death, the Transformation. In a modulo twelve system, as is used in analog clocks, thirteen is congruent to one.
